The Challenge
Define a research strategy to support Lowe's ambition to lead in Blinds & Shades — uncovering customer barriers to confidence, conversion, and a seamless omni-channel experience.
Context

Why a category-specific research model

Appliance and home décor are critical to Lowe's seasonal revenue, but research had historically been treated as a horizontal function — applied evenly across product surfaces regardless of category nuance. I championed a different approach: foundational research designed to surface the breakdowns that only show up when you look at one category deeply.

The work needed to do two things: shape the Q3 roadmap for Blinds & Shades, and prove out a research model that could be scaled by other teams.

Research Goals
  • Align cross-functional priorities and reduce churn risk by understanding how customers shop for blinds & shades
  • Identify usability and decision-making gaps in the online journey to optimize conversion
  • Build a research model the org could scale to other categories

Key Insights

Three insights that reshaped the roadmap

Product Education Gap. Customers felt boxed into choosing blinds without understanding key differences between types — contributing to uncertainty and drop-off.

Customization Visibility Barrier. Lack of clear dimension options deterred shoppers interested in custom blinds, limiting purchase confidence.

Overwhelming Configuration Experience. The configurator was perceived as confusing and time-consuming, creating friction in the final stages of purchase.

"I would expect to see more options. I can choose from something I can get when shopping in-store now, and that's expensive." — Survey respondent, customization barrier
— Cross-Functional Prioritization Workshop
Workshop Output
Workshop output: Used cross-team breakout boards to frame customer pain points into actionable opportunities. The Design Vision Workshop aligned UX, Product, and Merchandising on the two high-impact experience gaps that directly shaped the product roadmap — with examples pulled from real user flows for context.
What Shipped

How my research shaped the final customer experience

Unified Product List Page. Custom and stock products on a single page, letting users explore the full range without committing to a product type upfront. Three tailored CTAs and supporting context made the differences clear, setting expectations before the PDP.

Reordered Configurator. "Mount Type" expanded first to anchor expectations and reduce confusion with unfamiliar terms. Condensed layout minimized scrolling while keeping selections reviewable. Usability testing surfaced a minor interaction refinement around row-expand behavior.

Strategic precedent. The category-specific research model I championed was later scaled by other research and design teams, elevating UX as a strategic priority across Product and Merchandising functions.

Strategic Reflection

Research as a strategic lever, not a service

The model mattered more than the findings. The work that resonated most across the org wasn't the usability discoveries — it was demonstrating that how we did research, focused at the category level, was itself a competitive advantage. Other teams adopted the model because the outcomes spoke for themselves.

Keeping customer voice central. When workshops drifted toward opinion or assumption, I anchored the team back to direct customer feedback. The role of the researcher in cross-functional spaces is often to be the one who refuses to let the data get diluted.

Influencing beyond UX. The biggest indicator of the work's success: Product and Merchandising began incorporating category-specific research into their planning rhythms.
