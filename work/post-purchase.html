Work/Post-Purchase UX
Lowe's · Sr. Product Researcher (Lead IC)
2024 — 2025

Transforming the post-purchase experience to reduce churn and drive revenue growth.

Managing orders post-purchase is a critical, yet often overlooked, touchpoint for driving customer retention and loyalty. As sole embedded researcher for this portfolio, I made the case to leadership that post-purchase was both a risk and an opportunity — the first chance to re-engage customers and the last to build trust. My research surfaced clear pathways to improve loyalty, reduce service contacts, and preserve at-risk revenue.

The Challenge
Over half of online customers abandoned Lowe's within 12 months — a critical signal of post-purchase friction impacting retention, loyalty, and revenue.
Context

Reframing post-purchase from operational concern to growth opportunity

The post-purchase experience was viewed inside Lowe's as outside UX's influence — a logistics problem, not a strategic one. As the sole embedded researcher for the digital products portfolio, I set the research agenda independently and worked directly with Directors and VPs of Product to make the case for investment.

The work needed to do two things at once: solve the immediate churn problem, and shift how leadership thought about post-purchase as a growth lever.

Research Goals
  • Identify friction points in the post-purchase experience contributing to churn
  • Prioritize content and IA needs for an improved experience
  • Evaluate and refine design solutions to make repurchasing more seamless

Key Insights

What the data told us — and what the team had been missing

Fulfillment, customer service, and communication were the top three drivers of churn — not pricing or product quality, as the team had initially assumed.

Lowe's LTR scored -82.6 against competitors at +32.6: customers weren't leaving for cheaper options; they were leaving for clearer ones. DIY and Pro customers had fundamentally different needs being served by the same generic experience.

Defining the Problems

Two problems worth solving

Customers don't have the information they need. DIY customers don't feel fully informed about their orders, next steps, or item-specific support options — leaving them confused and helpless. This drives support calls and reduces loyalty.

Customers don't have a clear way to fix issues. DIY customers struggle to resolve order issues due to limited self-service options and unclear resolution paths, increasing reliance on support channels.

What Shipped

Research that shaped the final customer experience

Restructured Order Confirmation. Savings and loyalty points, prominent ETA, item imagery, editable delivery instructions, and fulfillment-specific next steps — all above the fold.

New "Fix an Issue" CTA. Captures customers who don't want to start a return — letting the business save the sale with flexible support solutions.

New Item Details sub-page. Consolidates protection plans, configured item details, return policies, and downloadable user manuals — in direct response to users who couldn't find product information after purchase.

Reframed roadmap. Post-purchase elevated to a strategic focus area for leadership going forward.

Leadership Reflection

How I grew as a leader on this initiative

Solving the right problem. On large-scale projects, it's easy for teams to get lost in detail. I learned the importance of continually steering the team back to the customer problems that delivered meaningful value to users and the business.

Influencing mindsets. The organization initially undervalued post-purchase. I learned to strengthen my advocacy — using data to highlight the value of retention over acquisition, and positioning this work as core to long-term growth.

Leading without authority. As the sole embedded researcher, I had no direct reports — but I had to influence designers, PMs, and engineering leads to act on findings. I learned to use psychological safety, clear documentation, and consistent storytelling as leadership tools when formal authority isn't available.

Owning the story. Storytelling is essential to UX leadership. Despite differing opinions on how to present the work, I maintained control of the narrative to ensure our research insights were communicated clearly, persuasively, and aligned to both user and business outcomes.
