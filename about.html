About

I help teams hear what their customers are actually saying.

Ten years in, I've learned the hardest part of research isn't the method. It's making sure the team is ready to act on the answer.

I'm Natasha. I'm a Lead UX Researcher who defines research agendas independently and influences product strategy at the Director and VP level. I specialize in translating ambiguous customer behavior into clear product direction — and in elevating research practice beyond individual study execution.

My career has spanned consumer goods and retail. I started at Procter & Gamble's Fabric Enhancers division in Cincinnati, leading longitudinal in-home research that connected consumer behavior to R&D formulation and brand positioning. I moved to the Grooming division in Boston, where I owned consumer research across four global innovation programs and a $100K+ research budget. In 2022 I joined Lowe's Digital Products (Charlotte, NC) as the sole embedded researcher for a portfolio serving millions of users, working directly with Directors and VPs to shape roadmap decisions and establish research standards adopted across the broader design org. I'm based in Miami, FL.

I came to UX research from a foundation in Chemical Engineering at the University of Florida. The analytical rigor still shows up in how I work — I'm comfortable with experimental design, behavioral segmentation, and quantitative validation — but ten years in, the hardest research problems are about narrative, not numbers: getting an organization to see what its own data has been telling it.

I lead with mixed methods, mentor cross-functional partners, and build research practices that outlast individual projects. I'm equally at home running a 12-week longitudinal ethnography and pressure-testing a hypothesis with rapid qualitative validation before A/B deployment. The method serves the question, not the other way around.

How I work — four principles.
— 01

Method serves the question

I start every project by asking what decision the research needs to drive — then choose the method that gets there fastest with appropriate rigor. Surveys aren't always the answer. Neither are interviews. The discipline is in the matching.

— 02

Customer voice over consensus

When workshops drift toward opinion or assumption, my job is to bring the data back into the room. Senior stakeholders are paid to have strong views — and the researcher is the one who refuses to let those views go unchecked.

— 03

Research as a system

Individual studies are easy. Building a practice that compounds across teams and years is harder — and more valuable. I think about repositories, ritual, and how the next person inherits the work.

— 04

Narrative is the final method

Insights only become decisions when they're communicated well. Storytelling is part of the craft, not optional polish on top of it. The work isn't done until the right people understand what the data is telling them.
